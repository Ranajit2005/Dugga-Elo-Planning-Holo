Durga Puja celebrates the victory of the goddess Durga over the demon king Mahishasura.
It begins on the same day as Navratri, a nine-night festival in many northern and western states of India(majorly celebrated in Bengal and Assam) that more broadly celebrates
the divine feminine(shakti).

Saraswati, Laksmi, Kartik, Ganesh are considered as the daughters and sons (respectively) of Goddess Durga.

Durga Puja’s first day is Mahalaya, which heralds the advent of the goddess (The Devipaksha starts ). Celebrations and worship begins on Sasthi, the sixth day. Lord Ganesh's
wife(Kolabou) is taken for an auspicios bathe in the ganga on Saptami,the seventh day. The main worship ritual is carried out on Asthami,the eight day in which the main attraction
is the Pushpanjali and Sondhey Arathi.

On Navami, the ninth day, the goddess and her children are worshipped for the last time and the devotees are served with an Amish(non veg) feast.

The celebrations end with Vijayadashami (“Tenth Day of Victory” ), when, amid loud chants
and drumbeats, sacred images are carried in huge processions to local rivers, where they are immersed. That custom is symbolic of the departure of the deity to her home and
to her husband, Shiva, in the Himalayas. Images of the goddess—astride a lion, attacking the demon king Mahishasura—are placed at various pandals (elaborately decorated bamboo
structures and galleries) and temples.
